The Governance Blind Spot

Executive Burnout:
What if Governance Were to Blame?

When exhaustion at the top becomes a systemic risk, boards can no longer afford to look away.

In the corridors of corporate power, a silent epidemic unfolds. Executive burnout—once dismissed as individual weakness or poor work-life balance—has emerged as one of the most underestimated risks facing modern organizations. Yet boards continue to treat it as a personal matter rather than a governance imperative.

The consequences of this blind spot ripple through entire organizations: impaired decision-making, strategic drift, toxic cultures cascading downward, and ultimately, shareholder value destruction. When a CEO or senior executive burns out, the damage extends far beyond one corner office.

The Maslach-Leiter Framework

For decades, researchers Christina Maslach and Michael Leiter have studied occupational burnout with scientific rigor. Their framework identifies six organizational factors that, when misaligned, create the conditions for burnout. Crucially, none of these factors are primarily individual—they are systemic.

The first factor, Workload, seems obvious: too much work leads to exhaustion. But the insight goes deeper. It is not merely volume but the mismatch between demands and resources that creates unsustainable pressure. For executives, this often manifests as impossible expectations with inadequate support structures.

Burnout is not caused by working hard. It is caused by working in an environment where systemic dysfunction makes recovery impossible.

Control—or its absence—represents the second critical dimension. Leaders who cannot influence the decisions that affect their work experience a corrosive sense of helplessness. Paradoxically, CEOs often have less autonomy than their titles suggest, constrained by boards, shareholders, regulators, and market expectations.

The remaining factors—Reward, Community, Fairness, and Values—complete the picture. When recognition is absent, when isolation replaces collaboration, when perceived injustice accumulates, when personal values conflict with organizational demands—burnout becomes not just possible but inevitable.

The Governance Imperative

If burnout is systemic, then its prevention must also be systemic. Individual resilience training, while potentially helpful, addresses symptoms rather than causes. Boards that genuinely wish to protect organizational performance must look at the structures they have created.

This requires asking uncomfortable questions. Has the board set realistic expectations for executive performance? Are support structures adequate? Does the compensation committee inadvertently incentivize unsustainable behaviors?

Five Questions Every Board Should Ask
  • 1
    Do we have visibility into the actual workload and stress levels of our executive team?
  • 2
    Have we created conditions where executives can acknowledge vulnerability without career risk?
  • 3
    Do our incentive structures reward sustainable performance or heroic overwork?
  • 4
    Is there a succession plan that does not depend on any single individual’s continued capacity?
  • 5
    When did we last genuinely discuss executive wellbeing as a board agenda item?

The answers may be uncomfortable. But discomfort is preferable to the alternative: discovering too late that the leader upon whom everything depended has reached a breaking point.
