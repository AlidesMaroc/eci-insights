EU Directive 970: Pay Transparency

A New Era for Gender Equality and Compensation Governance

On May 10, 2023, the European Union adopted Directive 2023/970, a landmark regulation designed to strengthen pay transparency and accelerate progress toward equal pay for equal work between women and men.

For HR leaders, board members, and executive search professionals, this directive represents both a compliance imperative and a strategic opportunity. Organizations that proactively embrace transparency will gain competitive advantage in talent attraction and retention.

This article examines the directive's key provisions and their practical implications, followed by an interview with legal expert Davide Sportelli on implementation strategies.

Preparing for Pay Transparency: A Legal Perspective

We spoke with Davide Sportelli, Partner at Simmons & Simmons, about how organizations should prepare for the new EU Pay Transparency Directive and the strategic implications for HR and governance.

What makes this directive different from previous equal pay legislation?

The fundamental shift is from reactive to proactive compliance. Previous frameworks required employees to prove discrimination; now, employers must demonstrate pay equity. The transparency requirements‚Äîpublishing salary ranges, reporting gaps, allowing employees to request comparative data‚Äîcreate systemic accountability that didn't exist before.

Which organizations will be most affected?

Large employers face the most immediate obligations, but the ripple effects will reach everyone. Companies with 250+ employees must report annually starting in 2027; those with 100-249 employees have until 2031. However, the prohibition on salary history questions and the requirement to share pay ranges apply regardless of size. Multinationals operating across EU jurisdictions face particular complexity.

What are the biggest compliance challenges you foresee?

Three stand out. First, defining "work of equal value"‚Äîthe directive requires objective, gender-neutral criteria, but many organizations lack robust job evaluation frameworks. Second, data infrastructure‚Äîcalculating and reporting pay gaps requires clean, comprehensive compensation data that many HR systems simply don't provide. Third, cultural resistance‚Äîtransparency often reveals uncomfortable truths that leadership may prefer to avoid.

How should organizations begin preparing now?

Start with a pay equity audit‚Äîunderstand your current state before the reporting requirements kick in. Map job roles against objective criteria to identify "work of equal value" categories. Invest in compensation analytics capabilities. Most importantly, treat this as a strategic initiative, not just a compliance exercise. Organizations that genuinely commit to pay equity will differentiate themselves in the talent market.

What role should boards and senior leadership play?

Governance oversight is essential. Boards should request regular briefings on pay equity metrics just as they do for financial performance. Compensation committees need to scrutinize not just executive pay but organization-wide equity. The directive creates potential personal liability for directors who fail to ensure compliance‚Äîthis needs to be on the board agenda.
