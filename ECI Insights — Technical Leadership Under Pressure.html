Technical Leadership Under Pressure

Why Talent Has Become a Governance Issue in Engineering & Construction

The Engineering and Construction sector stands at an inflection point. Accelerating infrastructure investment, the energy transition, and digital transformation are driving unprecedented demand for technical expertise—at precisely the moment when demographic shifts are depleting the talent pipeline.

For boards and executive committees, this is no longer a workforce planning challenge to delegate to HR. Talent scarcity in E&C has become a strategic constraint that shapes which projects can be pursued, which markets can be entered, and ultimately, which companies will thrive.

We spoke with Alessia Formato, Operations Director at AECOM Italy, about how the role of technical leadership is evolving—and what governance implications follow.

The Talent Equation

The global infrastructure gap is estimated at $15 trillion through 2040. Yet the engineering workforce is aging rapidly, with 25% of licensed engineers expected to retire within the next decade. The competition for remaining talent is reshaping competitive dynamics across the sector.

The Evolving Role of Technical Directors

We spoke with Alessia Formato, Operations Director at AECOM Italy, about how technical leadership is changing—and why executive search must adapt to a new reality in the Engineering & Construction sector.

How has the role of Technical Director evolved over your two decades in the industry?

When I started, technical excellence was the primary—almost the only—criterion. Today, a Technical Director must be a hybrid: deep technical competence remains essential, but it must be combined with project management skills, stakeholder management, regulatory expertise, and increasingly, digital fluency. The role has expanded dramatically while the pool of qualified candidates has not kept pace.

What makes talent acquisition particularly challenging in E&C right now?

Three converging factors. First, demographics—we're losing a generation of experienced leaders to retirement faster than we can develop replacements. Second, competition—tech companies, energy firms, and consulting groups are all pursuing the same profiles. Third, expectations—younger engineers want purpose, flexibility, and development opportunities, not just compensation. Companies that can't offer all three will struggle to attract and retain top talent.

You mentioned regulatory expertise. How significant is that dimension?

It's become critical. Environmental permitting, safety compliance, sustainability reporting—these aren't peripheral concerns anymore. A Technical Director who can navigate regulatory bodies, manage approval processes, and anticipate policy shifts is worth their weight in gold. This is especially true in complex projects involving multiple jurisdictions.

What should boards understand about technical talent that they often miss?

That it's a strategic asset, not a commodity to be sourced on demand. When a key Technical Director leaves, you don't just lose skills—you lose relationships, institutional memory, and often, client confidence. Boards need to treat technical leadership succession with the same rigor they apply to CEO succession. The talent pipeline should be a standing agenda item, not an afterthought.

How can executive search add value in this context?

By going beyond matching CVs to job descriptions. The best search partners understand the sector's dynamics, can assess both technical depth and leadership potential, and recognize cultural fit. They can also help companies articulate what makes them attractive to top candidates—which is often not what leadership assumes. In a scarce talent market, search is as much about selling the opportunity as finding the right person.

Looking ahead, what capabilities will define the next generation of technical leaders?

Beyond core engineering skills, I'd emphasize three things: digital fluency—not just using tools but understanding how AI and data analytics are transforming project delivery; sustainability literacy—the ability to integrate ESG considerations into technical decisions; and what I'd call "translation skills"—the capacity to communicate complex technical matters to non-technical stakeholders, including boards, investors, and communities.

Key Takeaways for Boards
1

Elevate technical talent to strategic agenda—scarcity is a governance issue, not just HR

2

Plan technical succession with CEO-level rigor and long-term horizons

3

Recognize the expanded skill set required: technical depth + digital + sustainability + communication

4

Partner with search specialists who understand sector dynamics and candidate motivations
